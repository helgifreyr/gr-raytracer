The Physics

Every pixel you see is the colour at the end of a single ray of light, traced backwards from the camera through curved spacetime until it hits the disk, falls into a hole, threads a wormhole, or escapes to the sky. Here's what's actually being computed.

Light follows the straightest possible path

In general relativity, gravity isn't a force — it's the curvature of spacetime, encoded in the metric gμν(x). Light always travels along a null geodesic: the straightest path available, which in curved spacetime is bent. So to render a black hole we don't apply a "gravity force" to photons — we integrate the geodesic equation and let the curvature do the bending.

This renderer works in the Hamiltonian form. A photon has a position q = (t,x,y,z) and momentum p, and its path obeys

dqⁱ/dλ =  gⁱʲ pⱼ
dpᵢ/dλ = −½ (∂ᵢ gᵃᵇ) pₐ p_b

The only thing that distinguishes one universe from another is the metric gᵃᵇ(x) and its derivatives. Supply those, and the same integrator renders any spacetime — that's what "metric-agnostic" means.

Black holes: the shadow and the photon ring

Near a black hole, light that passes too close is swallowed. There's a critical distance — the photon sphere — where light can orbit. Rays aimed inside the critical impact parameter spiral in and are captured; aimed just outside, they whip around one or more times and escape. The result is the shadow: a dark region surrounded by a bright photon ring, with progressively thinner, lensed copies of the sky piling up at its edge. The disk you see "above" and "below" the hole is the same disk — its far side, bent up and over the hole by gravitational lensing (the Interstellar look).

Spin: frame dragging (Kerr)

A rotating (Kerr) black hole drags spacetime around with it. Light co-rotating with the spin is deflected less than light going against it, so the shadow becomes asymmetric — a flattened "D" shape — and shifts to one side. Crank the spin slider and watch the shadow deform: that's frame dragging, a genuinely relativistic effect with no Newtonian analogue.

The accretion disk glows lopsidedly

Real black holes are ringed by hot, orbiting gas. Two relativistic effects shape how it looks, both folded into a single redshift factor:

  • Doppler beaming. The disk material moves at a large fraction of light speed. The side rotating toward you is beamed brighter (∝ δ³) and shifted bluer; the receding side dims and reddens. That stark left/right asymmetry is the most striking feature of a real black-hole image — and it flips if you reverse the spin.
  • Gravitational redshift. Light climbing out of the gravitational well loses energy, so the inner edge of the disk is dimmed and reddened relative to the outer edge.

(Viewed face-on the Doppler effect is mostly transverse — a mild, symmetric redshift — so tilt to an edge-on view to see the dramatic bright side.)

Horizons

The event horizon is the surface of no return. A ray crossing it can never come back out, so it renders black. For Schwarzschild it sits at the Schwarzschild radius; for Kerr it's smaller and depends on spin (r₊ = M + √(M²−a²)). A subtlety: a backwards-traced ray only asymptotically approaches the horizon — it takes infinite affine parameter to reach — so in practice we terminate on a thin shell just outside it.

The wormhole: a different beast entirely

The Thorne/Dneg wormhole (the one built for Interstellar) is ultrastatic — it has no horizon at all. Nothing gets captured. Instead, light either threads the throat into another universe or is lensed back into ours, decided purely by whether its impact parameter is smaller than the throat radius. Look through it and you see a second sky, ringed by the warped edge of the throat. Because the wormhole's topology is two sheets joined at a throat — which can't live in a single coordinate chart — it uses its own ray-tracing path, exploiting spherical symmetry to integrate a clean 2D problem per ray.

A note on units and honesty

Everything is in geometric units (G = c = 1), with mass M setting the scale. The integration is real GR — validated against the analytic light-deflection law (including its second-order term), Hamiltonian conservation, and the prograde/retrograde frame-dragging asymmetry. What's not physical is the disk's colour palette and brightness (chosen to look good) and the bloom; the geometry of the light bending is the real thing.
