The Physics

Every pixel is the colour at the end of one ray of light, traced backwards from the camera through curved spacetime. No "gravity force" is applied to the photons — we integrate the geodesic equation and let the curvature of spacetime do the bending. Here is that calculation, in full.

Spacetime, the metric, and null rays

Spacetime geometry is encoded in the metric $g_{\mu\nu}(x)$, which defines the invariant interval between nearby events,

$$ ds^2 = g_{\mu\nu}\, dx^\mu dx^\nu , $$

with coordinates $x^\mu = (t, x, y, z)$ and the sign convention $(-,+,+,+)$. Light travels on null geodesics: curves that extremise path length and satisfy $ds^2 = 0$. Massive observers move on timelike curves ($ds^2 \lt 0$); photons sit exactly on the light cone.

The Hamiltonian formulation

Rather than the usual geodesic equation with Christoffel symbols, this renderer uses the Hamiltonian form, which is both cleaner to integrate and the reason the engine is metric-agnostic. Treating the photon's position $x^\mu$ and conjugate momentum $p_\mu$ as phase-space variables, the super-Hamiltonian is

$$ H(x, p) = \tfrac{1}{2}\, g^{\mu\nu}(x)\, p_\mu p_\nu , $$

where $g^{\mu\nu}$ is the inverse (contravariant) metric. The geodesics are the flow of Hamilton's equations in an affine parameter $\lambda$:

$$ \frac{dx^\mu}{d\lambda} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_\mu} = g^{\mu\nu} p_\nu , \qquad \frac{dp_\mu}{d\lambda} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial x^\mu} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,(\partial_\mu g^{\alpha\beta})\, p_\alpha p_\beta . $$

Two things make this powerful. First, the only spacetime-specific ingredients are $g^{\mu\nu}$ and its first derivatives $\partial_\mu g^{\alpha\beta}$ — no Christoffel symbols are ever formed. Second, the value of $H$ is conserved along the flow, and for light it is pinned at

$$ H = \tfrac{1}{2}\, g^{\mu\nu} p_\mu p_\nu = 0 , $$

which is just $ds^2 = 0$ in momentum form. The renderer uses $H = 0$ to fix each photon's initial energy, and its conservation along the path is one of the things the test suite checks ($|H|/|p|^2 \lt 10^{-8}$ over the whole integration).

When the metric doesn't depend on $t$ (stationary) or on the azimuth $\phi$ (axisymmetric), the corresponding momenta are conserved — the photon's energy $E = -p_t$ and angular momentum $L = p_\phi$. Their ratio is the impact parameter $b = L/E$, which sets how close a ray passes and therefore whether it escapes or is captured.

Schwarzschild: the shadow

For a non-spinning black hole of mass $M$ (geometric units, $G = c = 1$), there is an unstable circular photon orbit — the photon sphere at areal radius $r = 3M$ — and a critical impact parameter

$$ b_{\rm c} = 3\sqrt{3}\, M \approx 5.2\, M . $$

Rays with $b \lt b_{\rm c}$ spiral in and are captured (black); rays with $b \gt b_{\rm c}$ swing around and escape. The boundary is the edge of the shadow. Far from the hole, a ray with impact parameter $b$ is deflected by

$$ \alpha = \frac{4M}{b} + \frac{15\pi}{4}\left(\frac{M}{b}\right)^{\!2} + \cdots $$

The leading $4M/b$ is the classic Einstein result; the renderer reproduces this and the second-order term (the validation suite checks deflection to better than 0.3%). Light that passes just outside $b_{\rm c}$ loops the photon sphere one or more times, producing the photon ring — exponentially thinner, lensed copies of the sky stacked at the shadow's edge.

Kerr: spin and frame dragging

A rotating black hole with spin $a = J/M$ drags spacetime into rotation around it. The renderer uses the horizon-penetrating Kerr–Schild form, whose inverse metric has the elegant structure

$$ g^{\mu\nu} = \eta^{\mu\nu} - f\, l^\mu l^\nu , $$

where $\eta$ is flat space, $l^\mu$ is a null vector field, and $f$ is a scalar built from the Kerr radius. The event horizon sits at

$$ r_\pm = M \pm \sqrt{M^2 - a^2} , $$

(the outer root $r_+$ is the shadow boundary). Frame dragging makes co-rotating light bend less than counter-rotating light, so the shadow becomes asymmetric — a flattened "D" — and shifts sideways. Crank the spin slider to watch it deform; the prograde/retrograde deflection asymmetry it produces is checked in the test suite.

The accretion disk glows lopsidedly

Hot gas orbits in the equatorial plane at relativistic speed. The observed brightness and colour are set by the total redshift factor $g = E_{\rm obs}/E_{\rm emit}$, which folds two effects together:

  • Relativistic Doppler beaming. Material rotating toward you is boosted; the emitted intensity transforms as $I_{\rm obs} = g^{3}\, I_{\rm emit}$, with the special-relativistic part $g_{\rm SR} = \big[\gamma\,(1 - \boldsymbol\beta\!\cdot\!\mathbf n)\big]^{-1}$ for orbital velocity $\boldsymbol\beta$ along the line of sight $\mathbf n$. The approaching side blazes and blueshifts; the receding side dims and reddens.
  • Gravitational redshift. Climbing out of the well costs energy, contributing a factor of the lapse $\alpha_{\rm lapse} = 1/\sqrt{-g^{00}}$, so the inner disk is dimmer and redder.

The cubic $g^3$ makes the beaming dramatic — the famous lopsided black-hole image. (Face-on, the Doppler shift is transverse and nearly symmetric, so tilt edge-on to see it.)

The disk's colour is driven by a Shakura–Sunyaev thin-disk temperature profile $T(r) \propto r^{-3/4}$, with the observed value scaled by the same factor $g$: $T_{\rm obs} = g\,T_{\rm emit}$. So the redshift that beams the brightness also moves the colour — the approaching inner edge runs hot and pale, the receding and outer gas cools and reddens. (The temperature is mapped through a stylized hot→cool palette rather than a literal blackbody curve: a real disk's temperature range is almost all pale white, which renders as a flat cream wash, so the palette exaggerates the contrast for legibility. The where is physical; the exact hue is artistic.)

Horizons and capture

The event horizon is the surface of no return; a ray crossing it renders black. A subtlety the code respects: a backwards-traced ray reaches the horizon only as $\lambda \to \infty$ — it asymptotes rather than arrives — so capture is detected on a thin shell just outside $r_+$, using a metric-derived criterion (the redshift $-g^{00}$ diverging, or the Kerr radius dropping below $r_+$), never a hard-coded radius.

The wormhole: no horizon at all

The Thorne/Dneg wormhole (built for Interstellar) is ultrastatic — its metric has $g_{tt} = -1$ everywhere and no horizon:

$$ ds^2 = -dt^2 + d\ell^2 + r(\ell)^2\,\big(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\!\theta\, d\phi^2\big) . $$

Here $\ell$ is the proper radial distance running from $-\infty$ (one universe) through the throat at $\ell = 0$ to $+\infty$ (the other). The throat shape is

$$ r(\ell) = \rho + W\!\left[x\arctan x - \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\!\big(1 + x^2\big)\right], \qquad x = \frac{2\,(|\ell| - a)}{\pi W} , $$

with throat radius $\rho$, half-length $a$, and lensing scale $W$. Spherical symmetry makes every ray planar, and the null condition reduces to a single radial equation,

$$ \left(\frac{d\ell}{d\lambda}\right)^{\!2} = 1 - \frac{b^2}{r(\ell)^2} . $$

This yields a clean dichotomy: if $b \lt \rho$ the right-hand side never vanishes, the ray threads the throat and emerges in the other universe; if $b \gt \rho$ it hits a turning point and is lensed back into ours. Look through the throat and you see a second sky, ringed by its warped rim — which is why the simulation lets you drop a different panorama on each side.

Units & honesty

Everything is in geometric units ($G = c = 1$), with $M$ (or $\rho$) setting the scale. The light bending is real general relativity, validated against the analytic deflection law (to second order), Hamiltonian conservation, frame-dragging asymmetry, horizon capture, and the wormhole dichotomy. What is not physical is the disk's turbulent gas texture, overall brightness, colour palette, and the bloom — chosen to look good (though the colour is indexed by the genuine relativistic temperature shift). The geometry of the rays is the genuine article.
